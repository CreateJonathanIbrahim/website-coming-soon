The Discovery Process: What I Ask Before I Touch Anything

Jonathan Ibrahim April 2026 6 min read

Most engagements fail before the first deliverable is produced. Not because of bad work — because of assumptions that were never tested, questions that were never asked, and expectations that existed only in someone's head. Discovery isn't a phase you complete and move past. It's a discipline you either have or you don't.

Over the years of working with clients on websites, operations systems, and digital strategy, I've developed a consistent process for the first moments of any new engagement. It looks different depending on the project, but the intent is always the same: build a shared map of reality with the client before anyone touches anything.

What Discovery Actually Means

Discovery gets described in a lot of ways — requirements gathering, intake, onboarding, kickoff. These are all fine words, but they all undersell what's actually happening. Discovery is the process of figuring out whether you and the client are looking at the same problem.

Most clients know what they want on the surface. They want a new website, a better system, a cleaner process. What they often haven't articulated — even to themselves — is why the current one isn't working, what success actually looks like, and what constraints exist that will shape every decision downstream. That's what discovery is for. Not to extract information. To build alignment.

The First Thing I Send Out

The first thing I do when a new client or project engagement begins is send out two documents: a stakeholder brief and a brand and goals exercise.

The stakeholder brief covers a wide range of questions about the business itself — history, current state, team structure, competitive landscape, and what the business has tried before. It gets into the current website: what it's doing for them, what it's not doing for them, where traffic comes from, and what visitors are actually expected to do when they arrive.

The brand and goals exercise focuses on the design and aesthetic side — visual preferences, brands they admire, tone of voice, how they want to be perceived. It helps separate "what we want the site to accomplish" from "what we want it to feel like," which are two very different questions that often get conflated.

A pattern I've seen repeatedly: many of the clients I work with built their own website at some point, often on a platform like Squarespace or WordPress, and have now reached a stage where they can afford — and genuinely need — something more professional and functional. That history matters enormously. Someone who built their own site has context. They've lived with limitations. They know what frustrated them. That existing knowledge shapes the whole conversation.

The One Question That Reveals Everything

If I had to pick one question that tells me the most about a client at the start of an engagement, it's this:

"What website have you seen that makes you think it's a good site — something you'd like to replicate?"

This question does more work than almost anything else I ask. The answer immediately tells me how much insight and knowledge the client has into web presence and digital experience. The range is extraordinary — and I mean that literally. On one end, you'll have someone who has spent time researching competitors, who can articulate why a specific site works, who has a Figma board of screenshots and knows what they like. On the other end, you'll have an HVAC contractor who doesn't own a laptop, who runs his entire business from his phone, and who has no reference point beyond "I want it to look professional."

The Diagnostic Value This question isn't really about taste — it's about digital literacy and the clarity of the client's vision. The answer determines how much education needs to happen alongside the work, how many reference points will be needed throughout the project, and how much translation will be required between what the client says and what the work actually needs to be.

Neither end of the spectrum is a problem in itself. The problem is not knowing which end you're dealing with before the project starts.

The Most Expensive Assumption in Any Engagement

There's a particular mindset I've seen derail more projects than almost anything else: the belief that things will sort themselves out naturally if you just get started.

It sounds reasonable on the surface. We're both smart people. We'll figure it out as we go. No need to front-load the process with a bunch of questions. Let's just start building and adjust from there.

What actually happens is this: every decision made without a clear picture of the goal becomes a potential revision down the road. The homepage gets designed one way, then the client sees it and realizes they wanted something completely different — but they couldn't have told you that without seeing the wrong version first. The feature gets built, then it turns out the client's definition of "simple" was not what any developer would call simple. The scope that seemed clear at the start expands in every direction because nobody locked down what was actually included.

"There's always an idea that if you don't say too much, what you want is just going to come naturally, or you'll figure it out along the way. Often that will cost a lot of time and effort and money in revisions, because you are so constantly adjusting for what's needed."

Discovery isn't overhead. It's the investment that makes everything downstream faster and cheaper.

More Information, Better Output

I don't call the stakeholder brief and brand exercise non-negotiable — because in practice, the work still gets done even when clients don't fill out every section. What I've found is that the quality of the output is almost directly proportional to the completeness of the input.

A client who thoughtfully answers every question gives me enough context to make good decisions independently — to choose the right image, write the right headline, suggest the right structure — without needing to check in at every step. A client who provides almost nothing forces me to either make assumptions or pause constantly to ask. Both paths slow the engagement down and introduce more risk.

The brief isn't about covering liability or generating paperwork. It's about giving the work the best possible chance of hitting the mark without burning unnecessary time on revision cycles that didn't need to happen.

If you're starting a project and wondering how to structure your own discovery process, I'm happy to share what the stakeholder brief looks like in practice. Reach out — it's one of those conversations that's useful regardless of the project type.
