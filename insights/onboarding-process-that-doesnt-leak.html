Building an Onboarding Process That Doesn't Leak

Jonathan Ibrahim April 2026 6 min read

Most onboarding problems don't announce themselves as onboarding problems. They show up later, disguised as communication issues, quality inconsistencies, or the slow realization that someone who's been on the team for three months still isn't operating independently. By the time it's visible, the damage is already done. The fix is almost always structural — and it almost always should have been built before anyone was hired.

When I built the ClickUp workspace at WISE Digital Partners, the tool architecture was only half the challenge. The other half was making sure every person who touched that workspace understood not just how to use it, but why it was built the way it was. That required building an onboarding process from the ground up — something that hadn't existed in a structured form before.

What "No System" Actually Looks Like

Before the new workspace was in place, ClickUp training at WISE was completely ad hoc. There was no unified method. No standard path. If you were new, you figured it out by watching someone else, by asking questions as they came up, or by trial and error inside a live workspace.

The symptom wasn't that people couldn't use ClickUp. Most people can click around a project management tool and get something done. The symptom was that everyone was using it differently. Statuses meant different things to different people. Tasks were structured inconsistently. Reporting that should have been automatic required manual cleanup because the underlying data wasn't clean. That's what a missing system looks like from the outside — not chaos, but quiet inconsistency that compounds over time.

Building the Training From First Principles

After the new workspace architecture was in place, I built a full ClickUp training program to accompany it. Not a document. Not a walkthrough video someone recorded once. A structured curriculum that covered the entire system from top to bottom.

The training runs through everything in a deliberate order:

  • Philosophy of use — why the workspace is structured the way it is, what problems it was designed to solve, and what principles should guide how you make decisions inside it
  • Notifications — one of the most commonly misconfigured areas; getting this right early prevents a lot of noise and missed updates downstream
  • Task flow — how work moves through the system from creation to completion, including what each status means and who owns each transition
  • Space-by-space overview — a full walkthrough of every Space in the workspace: what lives there, why it's structured that way, and how to navigate it

Each section includes a recorded video with narration, a written summary that covers the key points in text form, and a short quiz at the end. The quiz isn't punitive — it's a mechanism for confirming that the material landed before someone starts working in a live environment.

The One Thing Every Onboarding Checklist Includes That Doesn't Work

Watch the training videos.

It's on every onboarding checklist. It's almost never reliably done. The number of times I've seen someone mark that task complete without having actually watched the material is remarkable — and I genuinely have a hard time understanding the logic. The training exists to make the job easier. Skipping it makes the job harder. And yet.

The quizzes are the enforcement mechanism that changes this dynamic. When there's a quiz at the end of each section, you can't credibly claim completion without engaging with the content. A quiz isn't about catching people out — it's about creating a forcing function that ensures the material actually gets processed rather than skimmed.

The Structure That Makes Training Stick Video + written summary + quiz per section isn't over-engineering it. The video reaches people who learn by watching. The written summary gives them something to reference later. The quiz confirms retention before they go live. Each layer serves a different function, and removing any one of them weakens the whole.

How to Know When Someone Is Actually Ready

At the end of the training curriculum, there's a 30-minute live Q&A session. This is where I find out whether someone is actually ready to operate independently — and the signal is almost never what people expect.

"If they ask me good questions, unique questions, questions that reference things in the training — I know they've done the work. If they don't have any questions, I ask them to walk me through what their workflow is going to look like as soon as we hang up the call."

The quality of someone's questions tells you far more than their quiz scores. A person who has genuinely absorbed the material will ask questions at the edges — things the training covered but didn't fully resolve for their specific situation. They'll reference something they saw in the Space overview and ask how it connects to their daily workflow. They'll push back on something that doesn't quite match their mental model.

A person who hasn't really engaged will either have no questions (because they don't know enough yet to know what they don't know) or will ask questions that were directly answered in the training. Both are signals worth paying attention to.

The "walk me through your workflow" prompt is the most reliable final check. If someone can describe, coherently and specifically, what they're going to do in ClickUp starting Monday morning — what they'll open, how they'll find their tasks, how they'll update status, how they'll communicate through the task — then they're ready. If they can't, the conversation continues.

Onboarding is one of those things that always seems like it can wait until after the hire. It can't. The cost of an underprepared team member compounds quickly. If you're thinking through how to build this for your own team, I'm happy to talk through it.
