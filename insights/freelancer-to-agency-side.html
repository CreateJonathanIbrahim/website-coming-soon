From Freelancer to Agency-Side: What Nobody Tells You About the Switch

Jonathan Ibrahim April 2026 7 min read

There's a version of this transition that looks straightforward from the outside: bigger title, bigger team, more resources, less grinding solo. What nobody tells you is how fundamentally different the mental model needs to be. The skills that made you successful on your own are not the ones that will make you successful inside an organization — and realizing that quickly is the whole game.

I built Electrician Metrics from the ground up — a digital marketing operation focused specifically on trades businesses. I ran everything: the client relationships, the strategy, the production, the operations. Every problem that existed was mine to solve. Every solution had to come through me. Then I moved to the agency side as Head of PM at WISE Digital Partners, and the transition required a complete rethinking of how I operated.

You Are Part of a Machine, Not the Whole Machine

The biggest shift I had to make wasn't tactical. It was psychological. When you run your own operation, you are the machine. Every gear is yours. Every output runs through you. That's both the appeal and the exhaustion of it.

When you step into a leadership role inside someone else's organization, you become a part of a larger machine instead of the whole thing. That sounds obvious. In practice, it's harder to internalize than you'd expect.

"Something that I still struggle with is not needing to come up with every solution. Often times, when you put yourself in a position to come up with every solution, you run yourself entirely too thin — or you step into other people's scopes of work unintentionally."

Stepping into someone else's scope without meaning to is one of the quieter ways to damage trust on a team. It doesn't read as initiative — it reads as a lack of faith in that person's ability to handle their own work. Learning to hold back, to let others own their area even when you can see a faster path, is one of the real skills of organizational leadership.

The Translation Problem Nobody Warned Me About

I expected client work to get easier with more resources behind me. And parts of it did. What I didn't anticipate was how difficult it would be to translate client vision into something designers and developers could actually act on.

When you're solo, you can bridge the gap yourself. You hear what the client says, you understand what they mean underneath it, and you produce the work. There's no translation step — you are the translator and the executor.

At the agency level, that translation has to travel through other people. I pick up on nuances in client conversations — the hesitation in how they describe something, the thing they're reaching for but can't quite name, the aesthetic instinct behind an offhand comment. Getting that across to a designer or developer effectively is genuinely hard work, and I'd be lying if I said I had it completely figured out. It's something I work at constantly.

The mistake most people make is assuming that "here's what the client wants" is enough. It's almost never enough. The gap between what a client says and what they mean is exactly where projects go sideways — and bridging that gap is the PM's job, not the designer's.

What Was Easier Than I Expected

Client management. Without hesitation.

I had assumed that managing client relationships at a larger agency — with more moving parts, more stakeholders, more visibility — would be proportionally more complex. It turns out that the fundamental skill of communicating with business owners and helping them understand the process is fairly transferable regardless of the organizational size.

Business owners, by and large, are practical people. They want to know what's happening, why it's happening, and when to expect the next thing. If you can answer those three questions consistently and clearly, the relationship stays healthy. That part wasn't harder at the agency level. In a lot of ways, it was easier — because I had more infrastructure behind the promises I was making.

Earning Credibility When Nobody Knows Your Story

Every position I've moved into, I've had to earn credibility from scratch. No one walks into a new role with their reputation already loaded into the room. The people around you know your title. They don't know what you've built, what you've seen, how deep the experience goes.

"Most people see the position title and assume one thing without understanding the full scope of everything I've accomplished."
Two Things That Actually Build Credibility After doing this across multiple roles and organizations, the formula is simple — not easy, but simple. Go above and beyond what's expected of you, and do exactly what you say you're going to do. Just those two things, consistently applied over time, build more credibility than any background brief or LinkedIn profile ever will.

There's no shortcut here. You can't explain your way into credibility. You can't present credentials into it. You earn it incrementally, one kept commitment at a time, one result that exceeded expectations at a time. The process is slower than it feels like it should be — and then, at some point, it isn't a question anymore.

What I'd Tell My Freelance Self

The thing I've learned most clearly on the agency side is that the perception of value is something you can frame effectively through good work — not through a breakdown of your hours.

When I was freelancing, I defaulted to justifying my pricing by explaining how many hours I'd invested in a deliverable. I thought transparency about effort would signal value. What it actually did was anchor the conversation to cost instead of outcome. I was presenting myself as a vendor of time rather than a generator of results. That framing undercut me constantly, and I didn't fully see it until I was on the other side of it.

The other thing I think about often is how statements of work should have been structured on the freelance side. The SOW isn't just a legal document — it's a positioning tool. The way you describe scope, deliverables, and the value of your work in a SOW tells the client exactly how to think about what they're buying. I was too loose with that framing for too long, and I paid for it in ways that were completely avoidable.

This transition is one I think about often. If you're navigating something similar — freelance to in-house, solo to team lead — I'm genuinely happy to compare notes.
