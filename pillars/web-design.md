# Pillar: Web Design

## Core Concepts in This Territory

- **Service business websites are sales tools, not products.** The site exists to convert attention into a meeting, a lead, or a purchase decision. Architecture choices (which pages exist, which hierarchy they sit in, which conversion paths the design supports) follow how the business actually sells. Sites built around what looks balanced in a sitemap rather than how the business sells produce traffic without conversions.

- **Performance is a build-process problem.** Lighthouse scores, Core Web Vitals, and load times look like developer concerns. They are actually the output of how the team builds. A team with no performance discipline produces slow sites no matter how skilled the developers are. A team with performance baked into the build process produces fast sites consistently. Improving performance one site at a time is a symptom fix. Improving the build process is the real work.

- **The Figma to dev handoff at agency scale.** Most teams treat handoff as a single moment (designer finishes, developer starts). At agency scale with multiple sites in flight, handoff is a continuous interface that needs structure. Component naming conventions, asset organization, annotation patterns, and the moment design decisions get locked all materially affect dev velocity and revision count.

- **WordPress and Elementor as the practical agency stack.** WordPress gets dismissed by developers and overpraised by marketers. The realistic position is that for most service businesses at most price points, WordPress with Elementor produces faster builds, cheaper revisions, and easier client handoff than a custom build or a headless setup. The choice is not aesthetic preference. It is delivery economics.

- **Build process redesign as a leverage point.** Most websites get built using whatever process the agency happened to settle into. That process is rarely optimized. Redesigning it (where reviews happen, what gets templated, where the QA pass sits, how revisions get batched) produces compounding efficiency gains. The 26% efficiency improvement at TGSG came from this kind of redesign, not from individual technique improvements.

- **Multi-site QA at portfolio scale.** Quality on one site is a checklist problem. Quality across 20+ concurrent sites is a process problem. The QA pass that works on a single site usually does not survive at portfolio scale, because the bottleneck shifts from "did we check it" to "did we check it consistently across every site this week."

- **Design and dev coordination as the central problem.** Most website failures are not design failures or dev failures. They are coordination failures dressed as one or the other. The PM-shaped instinct (where do handoffs break, what context gets lost between functions) is what turns a competent design team and a competent dev team into a delivery system that consistently ships.

## What the Audience Gets Wrong

**That design and dev are separate phases.** They are a coordination problem. The team that treats them as sequential phases produces handoff failures every project. The team that designs the interface between them produces consistent output.

**That a pretty site is a working site.** Visual quality is necessary, not sufficient. A site that looks good and converts nothing is a more expensive failure than a site that looks fine and books calls.

**That performance is a developer problem.** Performance is the output of how the team builds. Treating it as a single-developer skill produces uneven sites. Treating it as a build-process discipline produces consistent ones.

**That WordPress is a beginner stack.** WordPress at agency scale, with the right templating discipline and deployment workflow, produces faster client handoffs and lower lifetime revision costs than most custom builds. The dismissal is usually from people who have not run a portfolio at the relevant volume.
