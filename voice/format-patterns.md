# Format Patterns: Personal Brand Site

Structural guidance for the personal brand site at jonathanibrahim.com. This file answers how content gets organized: not what to say, but how to organize what gets said.

Voice files (`constraints.md` and `tone-and-style.md`) apply to all content under Jonathan's name. This file specifies how the voice bends for the personal brand site specifically.

> Format guidance for other surfaces (LinkedIn carousels and posts, resume, cover letter, outreach email) lives in JonathanOS, not in this repo.

---

## Personal brand site

Stance: the trusted tech friend who is the expert in everything and can explain it easily. Warmer than LinkedIn. More human than a resume. More authoritative than a blog.

The site has five section types, each with its own pattern.

### Home

Single page. Answers three questions in order: who is Jonathan, what does he do, why should the visitor stay.

- **Above the fold:** a one-line positioning statement in plain language, not a title. Not "Head of Project Management." Something that names the translation skill in concrete terms.
- **Proof band:** three to five specific results (metrics, named companies, named outcomes). No logos-as-decoration. Numbers with context.
- **What he does (short):** two or three sentences naming the kinds of problems he solves. Specific enough that a business owner reading it knows whether to click further.
- **Primary CTA:** one clear next step. Read the case studies, read the insights, or book a call. One, not three.

### About

First person, long-form. Reads like a conversation with someone who happens to be very good at what he does. The About page is a narrative, not a chronological job list.

Structure:

- **Origin beat:** how he ended up across dev, PM, marketing, AI. The range is the story.
- **Current lens:** what he spends his days doing now and why it matters.
- **What he cares about:** systems over tactics, translation over specialization, evidence over frameworks.
- **What he does not do:** explicit filtering, so visitors self-select in or out.
- **Personal note:** one short paragraph signaling the human behind the work. Not a hobby list. Something that shows character.

Length: 400-800 words. Short enough to read, long enough to land.

### Services

Describe the work in terms of the outcome, not the deliverable. Each service entry has:

- **Name:** specific. "ClickUp infrastructure build" is better than "operations consulting."
- **Who it is for:** one sentence naming the company stage or the problem shape.
- **What Jonathan does:** three to five concrete actions.
- **What the client gets:** the measurable outcome.
- **Proof:** one specific past result, with numbers.

No pricing unless the price is itself a positioning statement. No feature lists.

### Case studies

One per project. Long-form. These are the load-bearing content for the site.

Structure:

- **Headline:** the outcome in one sentence, with the metric.
- **Context:** company, industry, scale, the problem at the start.
- **The decision:** what Jonathan chose to do and why, including what he chose not to do.
- **The system:** the specific mechanism he built. Screenshots, diagrams, or concrete examples where possible.
- **What changed:** before/after with real numbers. No vanity metrics.
- **What he would do differently:** one honest reflection. Shows the thinking alongside the win.

Length: 800-1500 words.

### Insights

The site's blog. Distilled longer-form pieces, often originating from material developed in adjacent surfaces. Tone sits between sharp single-claim posts and systematic, specific case studies.

Structure per post:

- **Title:** a claim, not a topic. "Most CLAUDE.md files are written as documentation. They should be written as instructions." is a title. "How to write CLAUDE.md files" is a topic.
- **Opening:** no throat-clearing. First sentence makes a claim or names a specific observation.
- **Body:** argument with evidence. 800-1500 words, follows the same discipline on specificity as a case study.
- **Closing:** a single observation that shifts how the reader thinks about the argument.
