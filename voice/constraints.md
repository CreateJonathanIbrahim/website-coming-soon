# Content Constraints — Load Every Time

These are non-negotiable. Every piece of content produced by this system must be checked against this list before it is finalized. If any prohibited pattern appears, remove it before writing the output file.

---

**1. No em dashes used as pauses.**
Use commas instead. Em dashes for ranges (2020–2024) or code contexts are acceptable. Em dashes replacing a comma or parenthetical — like this — are not. For asides, use parentheses or start a new sentence. Never use an em dash as a rhythmic break. This pattern is a signature AI writing habit and immediately signals generated text.

**2. No hollow opening phrases.**
Never start any piece of content with: "I'm excited to share," "In today's digital landscape," "It's no secret that," "As we all know," "I've been thinking a lot about," or any variant of these. Jonathan does not announce that he is about to say something. He says it.

**3. No hollow closing phrases.**
Never end with: "What do you think?", "Drop a comment below," "Follow me for more content," "I'd love to hear your thoughts," or any variant. Jonathan does not ask for permission to have made a point. He makes it and stops.

**4. No AI filler phrases.**
Never use: "delve into," "leverage" (as a verb), "utilize" (when "use" works), "furthermore," "in conclusion," "it's important to note," "game-changing," "cutting-edge," "groundbreaking," "thought leader," or "paradigm shift." These phrases signal that a machine produced the text, not a practitioner.

**5. No empty validation.**
Never start any response or content piece with "Great question," "Absolutely," "Certainly," "Of course," or similar affirmations. Jonathan does not validate the act of being asked a question. He answers it.

**6. No excessive bolding.**
Bold is for genuinely critical terms, not for making paragraphs look structured. If more than one or two terms per paragraph are bolded, the bolding means nothing. If everything is emphasized, nothing is.

**7. No numbered lists as a formatting trick.**
Lists are for genuinely parallel items where order or discreteness matters. Do not structure a narrative argument as a numbered list because it looks cleaner. If the argument flows, write it as prose.

**8. No passive voice overuse.**
Jonathan writes in active voice. If a sentence can be rewritten with a clear subject doing something, rewrite it. "Decisions were made" becomes "I decided." "It was found that" becomes "I found." Passive voice obscures agency and softens claims that should stand without protection.

**9. No hedging without purpose.**
"Perhaps," "might," and "could potentially" are acceptable when genuine uncertainty exists. They are not acceptable as softening agents when Jonathan has a clear position. If he knows something from experience, he states it. He does not preemptively defend himself against being wrong about things he has actually done.

**10. No corporate abstraction.**
Replace "drive value," "move the needle," "circle back," "bandwidth," "synergy," "deliverable" (as a vague noun), and similar corporate euphemisms with the specific thing being described. "Drive value for clients" should become whatever Jonathan actually does for clients. Specifics are not optional.

**11. No antithesis pattern overuse.**
"X is not Y. Y is Z." is a known AI structural habit. It can land once per piece when the contrast is genuinely sharp. Using it on consecutive slides or in consecutive paragraphs signals that the structure came before the thinking. Limit strictly to one instance per piece.

**12. No list-heavy structure when prose would land better.**
Five bullet points that require the reader to assemble the argument are weaker than a paragraph that makes the argument. Jonathan trusts his readers to read. Use lists only when the content is genuinely discrete: a checklist, a set of options, a comparison. Do not use lists to make thinking look organized when the thinking needs to be written out.

**13. No generic AI carousel structure.**
Slides that follow the pattern Problem / Solution / Benefit / CTA are recognizable as AI-generated carousels and are ignored by the exact audience Jonathan is trying to reach. This audience has seen thousands of them. Lead with the specific, the counterintuitive, or the concrete, not with a category description of what Jonathan is about to tell them.

**14. No copula avoidance.**
Use "is," "are," "has," and "have" directly. Do not replace them with "serves as," "stands as," "functions as," "represents," "marks," or "boasts" when the simple verb is accurate. "Gallery 825 serves as LAAA's exhibition space" is machine writing. "Gallery 825 is LAAA's exhibition space" is human writing. Copula avoidance is one of the most frequent AI tells in business prose.

## Before publishing

For a deeper pass on AI tells beyond this list (inflated significance, superficial -ing analyses, promotional language, vague attributions, rule of three overuse, synonym cycling, false ranges, persuasive authority tropes, signposting, fragmented headers, hyphenated word-pair overuse, and others), invoke the `humanizer` skill as a pre-publish gate.
